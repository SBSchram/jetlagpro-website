Why Your Body Clock Gets Confused When You Travel

August 3, 2025 • By Dr. Steven Schram, Licensed Acupuncturist

If you've ever wondered why crossing time zones leaves you feeling like you've been hit by a truck while your phone cheerfully adjusts to the new time, you're not alone. The answer lies deep within your cells, where an ancient biological clock is desperately trying to keep up with modern jet travel.

Your Body Has Multiple Clocks (And They Don't Always Agree)

Most people think of jet lag as simply being tired at the wrong time. But the reality is far more complex. Your body doesn't have just one clock – it has thousands of them, and they all need to be reset when you travel.

At the center of it all is your suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) – a tiny cluster of about 20,000 neurons in your brain that acts as your master timekeeper. Think of it as the conductor of an orchestra, trying to keep all the different sections playing in harmony.

But here's where it gets interesting: every major organ in your body has its own molecular clock. Your liver, stomach, heart, and kidneys all have their own sense of time, based on the same clock genes (Per1, Per2, Clock, Bmal1) that drive rhythmic activity throughout your body.

The Problem: Different Clocks Reset at Different Speeds

When you fly across time zones, these clocks don't all adjust at the same rate:

  • Your brain's master clock (SCN) responds primarily to light and can take 3-5 days to fully adjust
  • Your liver clock responds more to meal timing and may adjust faster or slower depending on when you eat
  • Your digestive system has its own rhythm that can take up to a week to synchronize
  • Your hormone production (cortisol, melatonin) follows yet another timeline

This is why you might feel hungry at 3 AM but wide awake at bedtime, or why your digestion feels off for days after travel. Your internal orchestra is playing different songs at the same time.

Why Traditional Solutions Often Fall Short

Most jet lag advice focuses on just one or two aspects of this complex system:

Light therapy helps reset your master brain clock, but does little for your peripheral organ clocks.

Melatonin can help with sleep timing, but it's a blunt instrument that doesn't address the underlying circadian disruption in your organs.

Meal timing can help reset some organ clocks, but misses the neurological components entirely.

Where Timed Acupressure Fits

This is where timed acupressure may add something useful. Traditional Chinese medicine described a 24-hour rhythm in which different body systems peak at predictable times.

Research on acupuncture points has reported effects on several body systems at once:

  • Neurological effects: Point stimulation may influence neurotransmitters involved in sleep-wake cycles
  • Hormonal effects: Acupressure has been studied for effects on cortisol and melatonin rhythms
  • Timed schedule: Each horary point aligns with a two-hour window on destination time

Pressing the right points on that schedule (roughly every two hours during travel) gives you a structured routine alongside light, sleep, and meal timing.

The Bottom Line

Jet lag isn't just about being tired – it's about temporarily disrupting one of the most fundamental processes in your body. Understanding this complexity helps explain why simple solutions often fail, and why a destination-time routine like timed acupressure can complement light, sleep, and meal timing.

The next time you travel across time zones, remember: you are not just fighting sleepiness. You are helping many internal clocks adjust to a new rhythm. A coordinated routine can support that process alongside light, meals, and sleep.
