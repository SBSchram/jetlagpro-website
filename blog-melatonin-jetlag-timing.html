Melatonin Timing for Jet Lag: What Travelers Should Know

May 30, 2026 • By Dr. Steven Schram, Licensed Acupuncturist

Melatonin is one of the most discussed jet lag supplements—and one of the most misused. It is not primarily a sedative; at low doses it can act as a darkness signal that helps shift your circadian clock when timed near local bedtime. This article summarizes common research-based guidance in plain language. It is not medical advice; ask your clinician before starting any supplement.

Melatonin as a Clock Signal, Not a Knockout Pill

Your pineal gland normally releases melatonin in the evening as light fades. Taking exogenous melatonin at the wrong circadian phase may do little for jet lag—or can increase grogginess without shifting the clock. Sleep medicine writers often emphasize that timing matters more than brand, and that many over-the-counter products are dosed higher than phase-shifting studies use.

Dose: Lower Than Many Bottles Suggest

Research reviews and travel-medicine summaries frequently cite roughly 0.3 mg to 1 mg near destination bedtime for eastward adjustment. Retail products at 5–10 mg are common in the U.S.; those doses may help some people fall asleep but are not always what circadian studies use for clock shifting, and they can cause next-day fog in sensitive travelers.

If you and your clinician agree to try melatonin, starting low and holding timing steady for a few nights is a conservative approach discussed in many patient-facing guides.

Eastward vs Westward Use

Eastward (advance your clock)

  • Melatonin is most often discussed for eastward trips, when local bedtime arrives before you feel sleepy.
  • Typical pattern in educational materials: take a low dose about 30–60 minutes before your target local bedtime on arrival night and sometimes for two to four nights after.
  • Pair with morning outdoor light (see light exposure guide) rather than relying on melatonin alone.

Westward (delay your clock)

  • Many travelers need little or no melatonin westward—the challenge is staying awake until local bedtime, not falling asleep too early.
  • When used, it is usually near local bedtime for a short course, not during the day when you need alertness.

What Melatonin Does Not Replace

  • Light timing—still the dominant zeitgeber for the master clock
  • Meal and sleep scheduling on local time
  • Hydration and alcohol moderation on long flights
  • Structured routines such as timed acupressure on destination time if you use JetLagPro

Interactions and Who Should Ask First

Melatonin can interact with sedatives, anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, and other medications. Pregnancy, breastfeeding, epilepsy, and autoimmune conditions warrant professional guidance. In many countries melatonin is prescription-only or regulated differently than in the U.S.
