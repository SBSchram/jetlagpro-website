The Chinese Organ Clock

According to Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), each of the twelve meridians has a daily two-hour window when it is most active, known as "Horary Time." This creates a 24-hour cycle where different organ systems are energetically dominant at specific times.

The most fascinating aspect of this system is that when you stimulate the horary point associated with your destination time—rather than your current time—you help your body begin syncing with the new time zone in advance. This technique signals your internal clock to shift from the departure time zone toward your destination's rhythm.

How This Works for Jet Lag

When you travel across time zones, your body's internal clock (circadian rhythm) remains synchronized with your departure time zone. By stimulating the horary points associated with your destination time zone, you're essentially "telling" your body what time it should be at your destination.

For example, if you're flying from New York to London (5-hour time difference), and it's 3:00 PM in New York but 8:00 PM in London, you would stimulate the point that's active at 8:00 PM (Pericardium-8) rather than the point active at 3:00 PM (Small Intestine-5).

This technique helps your body begin the process of adjusting to the new time zone before you even arrive, potentially reducing the severity and duration of jet lag symptoms.

Traditional Points vs. Travel-Optimized Points

The original research and classical TCM literature use a set of traditional horary points, many of which are on the feet or behind the knees. In cramped airline seats, these locations can be hard or impossible to reach safely. For the JetLagPro app, we use alternative points on the same meridians and time windows that are easier to find and stimulate while seated, so you can still follow the protocol in economy class.

Below you can see, for each affected time window, the traditional horary point alongside the JetLagPro travel-optimized point. The descriptions are kept in the same format that the app uses: first where to find the point, then how to stimulate it.

Traditional vs Travel-Optimized Horary Points

9–11 AM — Spleen Meridian

Traditional point — Spleen-3 (SP-3), \"Supreme White\"

Location: This point is along the inside edge of your foot where a big bump starts before you reach the big toe.

Stimulation: Use your thumb to press back and forth into this point. It should feel like you are rubbing into the bone and will likely be sore.

JetLagPro travel-optimized point — Spleen-10 (SP-10), \"Sea of Blood\"

Location: Grasp your thigh with your hand and thumb. Slide your thumb down until it naturally stops above the knee. It should be a couple of inches above the kneecap on the inside of the leg.

Stimulation: You can do both legs at the same time. Press into the point with your thumbs and rub the point with a circular motion. If you are in the right location, the point will be tender.

3–5 PM — Bladder Meridian

Traditional point — Bladder-66 (BL-66), \"Valley Passage\"

Location: On the outside edge of the foot where the small toe meets the larger bone.

Stimulation: Use your index or middle finger to rub into the base of the big bone. It may be sore so be both firm and gentle.

JetLagPro travel-optimized point — Bladder-2 (BL-2), \"Collected Bamboo\"

Location: At the inner end of your eyebrow. There's a small depression there that will likely be tender.

Stimulation: Use your index fingers to press the inside edge of both eyebrows at the same time. Rub them up and down, in and out. Focus on the tender points.

5–7 PM — Kidney Meridian

Traditional point — Kidney-10 (KI-10), \"Yin Valley\"

Location: While sitting, find the sore spot at the top of your bottom leg as shown in the video.

Stimulation: Rub your thumb around this area. It will be sore; don't press too hard. Apply a firm pressure as you press into the point using a gentle press-and-release method.

JetLagPro travel-optimized point — Kidney-27 (KI-27), \"Shu Mansion\"

Location: Find your collarbone. Slide your index finger inward until you are about 2 inches from the midline. It's in the depression just below the collarbone.

Stimulation: Use the index or middle finger of the opposite hand to press the point firmly. Find the tender point and rub it firmly or use a press and release technique. Do one side, then the other.

11 PM–1 AM — Gall Bladder Meridian

Traditional point — Gall Bladder-41 (GB-41), \"Foot Falling Tears\"

Location: With your foot on your knee. The point is between the small and fourth toe at the top of the first depression you find when moving up from the toes.

Stimulation: Press into the depression and rub up and down while maintaining a steady pressure.

JetLagPro travel-optimized point — Gall Bladder-20 (GB-20), \"Wind Pool\"

Location: With your thumbs, find the base of your skull. Find the hollow notch that is about 2 inches from the center.

Stimulation: Press your thumbs firmly into the hollow space and rub into the point. Press upward and slightly toward the center. Hold with a steady pressure.

1–3 AM — Liver Meridian

Traditional point — Liver-1 (LIV-1), \"Big Mound\"

Location: On the big toe, on the inside edge, about 0.1 inch from the corner of the nail.

Stimulation: Use your index finger to press into this point and rub it around with a firm pressure.

JetLagPro travel-optimized point — Liver-8 (LIV-8), \"Spring at the Bend\"

Location: Cross your leg. Slide your thumb down the inside of the thigh towards the knee. The point is in the depression in the crease above the inner side of the knee.

Stimulation: With your leg crossed, use your thumb to press firmly into the depression. Rub in a circular motion or hold steady pressure. Do one side, then the other.
