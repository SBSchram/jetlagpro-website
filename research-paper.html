Jet Lag Pro: A Mobile Platform for Evaluating Chronoacupuncture as a Tool for Jet Lag Management

Steven Schram PhD, DC, LAc

© 2026 Steven Schram. All rights reserved.

Abstract

Background: Jet lag is common among long-haul travelers, and existing strategies (light exposure, melatonin, behavioral scheduling) show inconsistent real-world effectiveness. Non-photic zeitgebers can influence circadian phase; repeated timed somatosensory stimulation aligned with destination time has been proposed as a potential cue but not quantitatively evaluated in travelers.

Objective: To assess whether adherence to a structured timed acupressure protocol delivered via a mobile app is associated with lower jet lag symptom severity across typical long-haul travel patterns.

Methods: In a prospective, app-based observational cohort, long-haul travelers used JetLagPro to receive timed prompts for a 12-point acupressure protocol aligned with the destination time zone. After travel, participants completed a jet lag survey based on the core domains of the Liverpool Jet Lag Questionnaire. We compared composite symptom scores across adherence levels and time zones crossed.

Results: PLACEHOLDER TEXT: Among [N] eligible trips (median [X] time zones; interquartile range, [Y–Z]), higher stimulation was significantly associated with lower composite jet lag severity (p < 0.001 for trend). Compared with minimal stimulation (0–2 points), high stimulation (9–12 points) was associated with a [Δ]-point lower composite score (95% confidence interval, [a–b]) and an effect size of approximately d ≈ [0.4–0.6]. Similar patterns were observed for eastward and westward travel and within the 6–8 and ≥9 time-zone bands.

Conclusions: PLACEHOLDER TEXT: In real-world long-haul travel, higher adherence to the timed acupressure protocol was consistently associated with meaningfully lower jet lag symptom scores and attenuated symptom escalation with increasing time zones crossed. While causal inference is limited by the non-randomized, self-selected design, the graded dose–response and robustness across sensitivity analyses support timed somatosensory stimulation as a promising, low-risk non-photic cue for jet lag management.

Significance: This study is the first systematic evaluation of timed somatosensory stimulation (acupressure) as a potential non-photic cue for jet lag in real-world travelers.

Introduction

Jet lag is a common problem for international travelers, arising from misalignment between endogenous circadian rhythms and the destination light–dark cycle.1 The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) and peripheral clocks re-entrain at different rates; natural adaptation after time-zone travel is roughly one hour per day for eastward travel and slightly faster westward, so that crossing many time zones can mean several days of impaired sleep, alertness, and well-being.

Current strategies to accelerate re-entrainment include timed light exposure, melatonin supplementation, and sleep or meal scheduling. These interventions are supported by phase-response data and are reflected in travel medicine guidelines, but adherence and real-world efficacy are variable. Light must be timed correctly to the destination schedule; melatonin is not equally effective for all individuals or directions of travel; and behavioral scheduling is often difficult to maintain during and immediately after long-haul flights.

Beyond light (the dominant zeitgeber), circadian phase can be shifted by non-photic cues such as exercise timing, meal timing, and behavioral activity.7 Repeated timed somatosensory stimulation is therefore a plausible candidate to facilitate re-entrainment, although it has not been systematically evaluated in travelers. The present study evaluates whether adherence to a timed acupressure protocol aligned with destination-time windows is associated with reduced jet-lag symptom severity in a prospective, app-based observational cohort.

Circadian Biology: Mechanisms of Internal Clocks

The scientific literature describes how circadian rhythms synchronize physiological processes to a 24-26 hour cycle through the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a cluster of neurons entrained by light.2,3 The SCN coordinates peripheral clocks in organs like the liver and heart, which regulate tissue-specific functions including vascular function and blood pressure regulation.4,5 Core clock genes (Clock, Bmal1, Period, Cryptochrome) drive a molecular feedback loop, producing rhythmic gene expression.6 Synchronization is maintained by Zeitgebers, external signals such as light, temperature, activity, and meals.7

Jet lag occurs when rapid time zone changes desynchronize these internal clocks (Figure 1). Non-photic zeitgebers—including exercise and feeding time—can influence SCN and peripheral clock phase in animal and human studies, supporting the idea that other timed sensory cues might contribute to re-entrainment.

Timed Acupressure as a Non-Photic Protocol

More than 40 years ago, Khoe8 and later Amaro9 proposed that targeted stimulation of acupuncture points during travel could help minimize jet lag by aligning the body with the destination time zone. Their approach drew on Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) principles documented as early as the Han Dynasty10 (200 BCE–200 CE). These texts describe a 24-hour cycle in which specific organs and their associated energy pathways (meridians) show heightened activity during recurring two-hour intervals (Table 1). Khoe and Amaro focused on a subset of points known as horary points, which correspond to each meridian’s elemental properties and are considered most influential during its peak activity window (Figure 2).

A recent review supports the broader concept that acupuncture may influence circadian biology, reporting effects on circadian clock genes, neurotransmitter systems, and sleep–wake regulation (Wu & Zhao, 2024).11 Although these studies primarily examined points associated with sleep, they suggest that acupuncture may act as a non-photic zeitgeber capable of modulating circadian rhythms.

Although the mechanistic pathways remain under investigation, these findings are consistent with the possibility that temporally targeted somatosensory stimulation may interact with circadian regulatory systems.

The protocol delivers repeated timed somatosensory stimulation aligned with destination local time, potentially acting as a non-photic circadian cue. The stimulation schedule is based on meridian activity windows from Traditional Chinese Medicine (Table 1, Figure 2); acupressure provides a structured delivery system for the cue.

Practical Point Substitutions

Classical protocols emphasize horary points. However, early testers traveling in economy-class seating reported that some foot and ankle points were difficult to access due to footwear or limited space. To improve practicality, the protocol was modified by substituting more accessible points along the same active meridian. The rationale is simple: stimulating a more accessible point on the active meridian during its designated window is preferable to omitting the meridian entirely.

This pragmatic modification aligns with broader TCM channel theory, which posits that stimulation anywhere along a meridian during its horary window can influence that channel and its associated organ system.

Although timed acupressure has long been advocated by practitioners for jet lag, it has not been quantitatively evaluated in large cohorts of real-world travelers. This prospective observational cohort study was designed to evaluate whether a time-aligned acupressure protocol shows signals of efficacy in real-world travel conditions. The study is intended as hypothesis-generating, with the goal of informing future randomized trials. We examined whether adherence to the meridian-aligned, time-specific stimulation schedule was associated with reduced jet-lag symptom severity compared with minimal use and historical baseline data.

The present study evaluates whether adherence to a timed acupressure protocol aligned with destination-time meridian windows is associated with reduced jet-lag symptom severity among long-distance travelers. We hypothesized that greater adherence to scheduled stimulation windows would correlate with lower post-travel jet-lag severity, consistent with a dose-response relationship expected for circadian entrainment cues.

Methods

Study design

This is an app-based prospective observational cohort study embedded in the JetLagPro mobile application. The app was freely available to the public without restriction.

Participants

Participants were self-selected travelers who sought and downloaded the app. This population is likely more motivated and health-conscious than the average traveler. While this does introduce a selection bias, it simultaneously enhances external validity for the group most likely to adopt such an intervention in real life.

Intervention protocol

The mobile application provides travelers with timing and instructions to stimulate specific points based on the destination time. Point selection follows the strategic substitutions described in Table 1. (The user can opt for the traditional points via a setting in the app.)

Ideally beginning at the airport (or any time thereafter), travelers select their destination. The app immediately identifies the first active point matching the destination’s local time, then schedules a full 24-hour sequence of prompts in two-hour increments. This process is illustrated below.

Scheduled reminders appear at appropriate times, prompting the traveler to complete a full 24-hour cycle. These reminders are persistent and remain on the traveler’s phone (or watch) until clicked. When clicked, the Points Screen (#3) shows the correct point image alongside a looping demonstration video. The watch also provides a “show me” option that displays the point image. Written instructions are provided above and below the phone images.

Point Images

After the trip ends, the app logs trip data to Firebase, including the arrival time zone to validate actual travel. One critical data element stored at trip end is the time-zone difference between origin and destination, calculated using IANA time-zone identifiers. The application automatically accounts for Daylight Saving Time when calculating offsets through the device’s native time-zone libraries. However, trips that begin or end during DST transitions may show a ±1-hour variance in reported time-zone counts, depending on when offsets are captured.

A final step at trip completion is scheduling a notification for 2 days later (a short interval chosen to minimize recall bias) to remind the traveler to complete the website survey. The survey is a streamlined assessment based on the Liverpool Jet Lag Questionnaire’s core symptom domains (listed below), using 1–5 severity ranges adapted for real-world mobile use as a single post-travel evaluation.

Outcomes

The post-travel survey is a streamlined, single post-travel assessment based on the Liverpool Jet Lag Questionnaire’s core symptom domains, using 1–5 severity ranges adapted for real-world mobile use. It retains the LJLQ’s core validated domains—sleep quality/disturbance, daytime fatigue, concentration difficulties, irritability/motivation, and gastrointestinal function—while omitting less central items (e.g., detailed appetite or bowel-frequency changes) to minimize participant burden.

The primary outcome is a composite jet lag severity score, calculated as the mean of the five domain ratings (each scored 1–5). Secondary outcomes are the individual domain scores and the within-subject difference between anticipated and actual composite scores.

Primary Research Question

Is adherence to the timed acupressure protocol as directed by the app associated with lower jet lag symptoms compared to minimal or no use?

This study did not include a randomized true control because the practical constraints of anonymous volunteer participation precluded randomization or a no-app sham condition. We therefore used a within-subject design in which each participant served as their own control. Participants voluntarily completed surveys that included two elements: (1) anticipated symptom severity (based on prior travel experience) and (2) actual post-travel symptom severity reported after the journey. The anticipated-versus-actual comparison was exploratory and subject to recall and expectation bias; the primary analytical strategy relied on between-adherence comparisons and an external baseline (Waterhouse et al.).

Baseline Data for Time Zones Crossed

Our analysis compared survey results with baseline severity data from Waterhouse et al.’s 2007 comprehensive quantitative assessment of jet lag severity stratified by time zones.1 In that study, non-interventional severity fell into the following ranges on a 1–5 severity scale.

  • 1-2 time zones: 1.8
  • 3-5 time zones: 2.5
  • 6-8 time zones: 3.1
  • 9+ time zones: 3.6

Stimulation Level Groupings ("adherence level")

Participants were grouped by the number of acupressure points marked as stimulated by the user in the app during travel:

  • 0-2 points (no intervention)
  • 3-5 points (low stimulation)
  • 6-8 points (moderate stimulation)
  • 9-12 points (high stimulation)

For each stimulation group, we analyzed post-travel symptom severity across time-zone categories to assess how stimulation level affected the expected symptom increase with greater time-zone crossing. This grouping provided sufficient sample sizes for statistical analysis while maintaining meaningful intervention categories.

Time Zone Offset and Travel Direction

The app recorded the time difference (offset), in hours, between origin and destination. A positive offset meant the body had to advance its rhythm (eastward), whereas a negative offset meant delay (westward). Eastward adjustment typically felt more difficult than westward adjustment. For example, Los Angeles to Sydney had a +19-hour offset, so the app classified it as eastward even though the flight traveled west geographically. For trips over 12 hours, the body could adapt by the shorter route around the clock, but the analysis used the direct arrival offset.

Statistical analysis

Covariate selection was guided by a directed acyclic graph (DAG) representing plausible causal relationships between travel characteristics, protocol adherence, and jet-lag outcomes (see project repository for reproducible figure code). Based on this framework, models adjust for the number of time zones crossed and travel direction (eastward vs westward), which may influence both adherence and symptom severity. We do not adjust for sleep timing or individual symptom domains, as these are likely on the causal path from adherence to overall severity (mediators) and adjusting for them would obscure the total effect of the protocol. Other potential confounders (e.g., trip duration, age, sex) are not included in the primary analysis.

We summarized trip characteristics across adherence groups using medians and interquartile ranges for continuous variables, and proportions for categorical variables.

Because individual travelers may contribute multiple trips, we used mixed-effects (multilevel) regression models with traveler identifier as a random intercept to account for within-person correlation. The primary analysis estimated the association between adherence category and post-travel composite jet-lag severity, with minimal adherence (0–2 points) as the reference group. Fixed effects included the number of time zones crossed (continuous) and travel direction (eastward vs westward). Adherence was also modeled as a continuous predictor (number of points completed) to improve dose-response resolution.

To evaluate potential dose-response effects, the number of stimulated points was modeled both as an ordinal variable and as a continuous predictor. Prespecified subgroup analyses stratified trips by travel direction and time-zone band (1–2, 3–5, 6–8, and ≥9 zones) to examine whether associations differed across travel patterns.

Several sensitivity analyses were performed to assess the robustness of the findings. First, we repeated the primary models using proportional-odds (ordinal logistic) regression for the composite jet-lag score to account for its ordinal 1–5 structure. Second, we refitted the models after excluding trips with missing or ambiguous time-zone or travel-direction information. Third, we restricted the analysis to one trip per traveler (first completed trip per device) so that observations were independent; this addresses the same repeated-measures concern via a subset analysis rather than a random effect. Fourth, we repeated the analyses after excluding trips that overlapped daylight saving time transitions where time-zone offsets are less stable.

For secondary outcomes, including individual symptom domains and anticipated-versus-observed symptom differences, we controlled the false discovery rate at 5% using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure. All statistical tests were two-sided with α = 0.05. Effect sizes (e.g., Cohen’s d) were reported alongside regression estimates to facilitate clinical interpretation.

Sample size and power. Assuming a medium effect size (Cohen’s d ≈ 0.5) for differences in composite severity between high- and minimal-adherence groups within a given time-zone stratum, a two-sided α of 0.05 and 80% power require approximately 128 participants per group. To allow for multiple adherence categories and covariate adjustment, we targeted at least 300–400 complete trips per major subgroup (e.g., high versus minimal adherence within the 6–8 and ≥9 time-zone bands).

Analyses were conducted in R. The analysis script and an export pipeline used to produce the trip-level CSV from Firestore are available on the project website (jetlagpro.com) for independent reproduction; the same pipeline was used to generate regression tables, effect sizes, and the severity-by-time-zone-and-adherence chart.

Preliminary Discussion

The proposed mechanism involves afferent pathways from somatosensory stimulation potentially influencing circadian physiology through neurohumoral pathways previously described in acupuncture research. We did not measure circadian phase or related biomarkers; proposed mechanisms remain speculative.

This paper presents a prospective observational cohort study of chronoacupuncture for jet lag in [loading...] long-distance travelers. Participants used the JetLagPro mobile app to self-administer acupressure at points aligned with destination time zones and completed a post-travel jet lag assessment.

Confounding variables

Traditional clinical trials face significant challenges in jet lag research because controlling travel variables, traveler demographics, and environmental factors is logistically complex.

Our app uses a naturalistic data-collection approach. It combines automated, objective logging of point-stimulation timestamps with a streamlined survey based on the validated core domains of the Liverpool Jet Lag Questionnaire.

This approach addresses many of those limitations while capturing real-world effectiveness across diverse travel conditions and large sample sizes.

The design prioritizes ecological validity and scalability over experimental control, reflecting how travelers actually use (or combine) interventions in practice.

We recognize that this methodology introduces potential confounding variables.

These include variable traveler compliance, possible placebo effects in tactile or app-based interventions, and subjective symptom reporting with a short 2-day recall window. They also include unrestricted use of concurrent strategies common among international travelers (e.g., melatonin supplementation, strategic bright-light exposure, caffeine, meal timing, and sleep-hygiene adjustments).

We did not collect data on these co-interventions. The primary aim was to evaluate the app’s chronoacupuncture protocol in unrestricted real-world behavior, rather than isolate its effect from all other influences.

Participants who complete more points may differ systematically in health behaviors and use of other interventions (e.g., melatonin, light). Therefore, we cannot separate the protocol effect from these factors (confounding by indication).

Without sham acupressure or blinding, we cannot distinguish specific effects of meridian timing from placebo or expectation. Five classical horary points were substituted for accessibility (Table 1), so the protocol differs from horary-only protocols. This difference may affect effect size if point-specific timing matters.

This pragmatic approach aligns with observational studies in travel medicine, where full experimental control is often infeasible. Even so, large-scale, prospectively logged adherence data and patient-centered outcomes remain highly informative for hypothesis generation and future dose-response exploration.

In this design, travel direction and number of time zones crossed are largely determined by the trip rather than by symptom expectations. Participants do not choose these factors based on anticipated jet lag, which limits confounding from these covariates compared with many observational settings.

This approach may inform CDC Yellow Book guidance on jet lag, which emphasizes timed light exposure, melatonin, and behavioral adjustments, and does not endorse acupressure because high-quality evidence is limited. We address that evidence gap by providing the first prospective, large-scale dataset on a structured chronoacupuncture protocol in real-world travelers.

This study used meridian-aligned points with substitutions for accessibility (Table 1). Whether the specific point or the timing is the primary driver of any benefit remains a question for future research.

Real-World Limitations and Variability

Travelers are not trained acupuncturists so point location and stimulation quality will vary during flights. Users may:

  • Mark a point as done without stimulating it
  • Stimulate something off-target
  • Apply too little pressure or have a shortened duration
  • Stimulate only one side when both sides are recommended
  • Miss points (e.g., while sleeping)
  • Forget to log a stimulation
  • Use other remedies (e.g., melatonin, sleep adjustments)

This variability is expected and intentional in our naturalistic design. We are testing whether app-guided self-stimulation of specific acupuncture points is associated with benefit for the average traveler under everyday conditions, rather than under idealized, lab-like execution. TCM theory holds that the entire meridian is active during its peak window, so even approximate or less-than-ideal stimulation at the correct time and nearby location should still provide some benefit.

The interdisciplinary framework that merges ancient temporal concepts with circadian biology creates a testable hypothesis from practices that have traditionally been anecdotal. If validated, chronoacupuncture could provide a non-invasive, cost-effective intervention accessible to travelers without medical supervision. Negative findings would redirect research toward alternative non-photic zeitgebers and inform scientific evaluation of other TCM temporal therapies.

Proposed Summary

Chronoacupuncture, tested through a mobile app, was evaluated for an association between timed acupressure at meridian-aligned points and adaptation to new time zones. The technique was examined for association with lower jet lag symptom severity and faster adaptation; findings remained hypothesis-generating until the prespecified sample size was reached and analyzed.

Ethics

This research was self-funded, conducted independently with no institutional affiliation, and the author declares no conflicts of interest. The research design posed minimal risk, as it involved self-administered acupressure, with participants free to engage at will. Survey and research data are tied only to an untrackable device ID; participant privacy was intrinsic. Data integrity safeguards include cryptographic signatures on trip records, immutable audit logging, and publicly accessible verification tools.
