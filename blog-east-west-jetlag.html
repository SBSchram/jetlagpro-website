Eastward vs Westward Jet Lag: What Changes and What Helps

May 30, 2026 • By Dr. Steven Schram, Licensed Acupuncturist

Travelers often say flying east feels worse than flying west. Sleep researchers generally agree: advancing your body clock (eastward) is harder than delaying it (westward). This guide explains why direction matters and what you can adjust—without promising a fixed recovery timeline.

The Core Idea: Advance vs. Delay

Your brain’s master clock can shift in two directions:

  • Phase delay — staying up later, sleeping later (typical westward challenge)
  • Phase advance — falling asleep earlier, waking earlier (typical eastward challenge)

Human biology tends to run slightly longer than 24 hours. Many people find it easier to stay up an extra hour or two than to force sleep an hour earlier. That asymmetry is why a red-eye from New York to London (eastward, +5 hours) often feels rougher than the return westward (−5 hours), even when the time difference is the same.

Eastward Travel: Common Patterns

When you fly east, local bedtime arrives while your body still thinks it is afternoon or evening. Typical symptoms include:

  • Lying awake when you want to sleep
  • Early-morning waking once partially adjusted
  • Low mood or irritability from sleep debt
  • Digestive timing that lags behind local meal schedules

Light: Morning light at your destination helps advance the clock. Seek outdoor brightness soon after local wake time; limit bright light in the late evening if you are trying to move bedtime earlier.

Melatonin: Research often uses small doses (roughly 0.3–1 mg) taken near destination bedtime for eastward trips—not as a sedative, but as a darkness signal. Timing matters more than brand; ask your clinician if melatonin is appropriate for you.

Sleep scheduling: Some travelers shift bed/wake times by 30–60 minutes per day for several days before departure. Even partial shifting can reduce the shock on arrival.

Westward Travel: Common Patterns

Westward flights usually ask you to stay awake longer relative to home time. Symptoms often include:

  • Heavy eyelids in the late afternoon or early evening locally
  • Difficulty staying awake until a reasonable local bedtime
  • Less severe insomnia on night one than eastward trips (for many people)

Light: Evening light can help delay the clock. If you arrive in the afternoon, outdoor light can support staying alert until local bedtime; avoid blasting yourself with bright light if you need sleep soon.

Melatonin: Westward travelers sometimes need little or no melatonin; when used, it is typically near local bedtime for a few nights. Again, individual response varies.

Naps: A brief nap (20–30 minutes) before local bedtime can take the edge off sleep debt without anchoring you to home time—but long naps often prolong adjustment.

How Many Time Zones Matter

Rule of thumb from circadian research: adjustment often scales with zones crossed, with eastward trips feeling disproportionately difficult beyond about three to four zones. Crossing the dateline or flying 8–12 hours off home time can mean several days of mismatch between sleepiness and local social schedules.

Practical Checklists

Before you fly east

  • Shift sleep earlier in small steps if your schedule allows
  • Plan morning outdoor light for day one at destination
  • Set phone/watch to destination time at boarding if that helps mentally
  • Install JetLagPro and pick your destination airport so the point guide starts in destination time

Before you fly west

  • Expect late-afternoon sleepiness on arrival; plan a short nap or activity, not a four-hour crash
  • Use evening light if you must stay up until local bedtime
  • Hydrate; limit alcohol on board—it disrupts sleep quality without speeding adjustment

First day on the ground (both directions)

  • Eat on local meal times when you can
  • Stay awake until a reasonable local bedtime if possible
  • Follow the app’s two-hour point reminders through travel and into the first day

When to Get Help

Persistent insomnia, chest pain, severe dehydration, or mood changes that worry you deserve professional evaluation—not a blog post. JetLagPro is for generally healthy travelers seeking structured self-care during trips.
