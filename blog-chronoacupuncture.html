What Is Chronoacupuncture?

May 30, 2026 • By Dr. Steven Schram, Licensed Acupuncturist

If you have searched for acupressure and jet lag, you may have seen the term chronoacupuncture—timed stimulation of specific acupuncture points based on the clock at your destination. This article explains what that means in plain language, where the idea comes from, and how the JetLagPro app applies it as self-administered acupressure (no needles).

A Simple Definition

Chronoacupuncture combines two ideas:

  • Chrono- (time): the schedule follows the destination time zone, not the time on your wrist at departure.
  • Acupuncture points: in this travel context, usually acupressure—firm pressure or tapping on the skin, not insertion of needles.

The method uses horary points: one key point on each of the twelve meridians, chosen because that meridian is considered most active during a particular two-hour window on the Chinese Organ Clock. During a long flight or after arrival, you stimulate the point that matches “what time it is there” roughly every two hours.

JetLagPro is built around that schedule: pick your destination, and the app reminds you which point to use for the current destination-time window, with videos and diagrams so you do not need prior acupuncture training.

Where Did This Come From?

Traditional Chinese medicine has long described a 24-hour cycle in which energy (Qi) moves through the twelve primary meridians in sequence. Classical texts such as the Huangdi Neijing describe organ systems as having peak activity at predictable times— the basis of the organ clock used in modern explanations of horary therapy.

In the late twentieth century, practitioners including Dr. John A. Amaro published jet-lag protocols that applied horary points to air travel: set a clock to destination time, and at each two-hour interval stimulate the point for the active meridian—typically on both sides of the body. The technique spread through acupuncture journals, seminars, and patient handouts long before smartphone apps existed.

What changed recently is delivery: travelers can use an app for timing, point location, and reminders instead of paper charts and mental math while exhausted at 35,000 feet.

How a Chronoacupuncture Session Works in Practice

Although details vary by teacher, the core steps are consistent:

  1. Set your reference clock to destination local time (24-hour format avoids AM/PM confusion).
  2. Identify the active meridian for the current two-hour block on the organ clock.
  3. Stimulate that meridian’s horary point—often with a fingertip, pen cap, or similar tool—on the left and right sides of the body.
  4. Repeat on the next two-hour boundary as destination time advances, through travel and often into the first day after arrival.

Practitioners emphasize that timing matters as much as point choice. Stimulating a point outside its window is a different intervention in horary theory. That is why JetLagPro anchors everything to destination time rather than home time.

How This Relates to Jet Lag and Circadian Rhythm

Jet lag is a mismatch between your internal clocks and local sun time. Light, meals, sleep, and melatonin are well-known zeitgebers— cues that tell the brain and organs what “time” it is. Chronoacupuncture proposes an additional cue: repeated, timed somatosensory input aligned with destination time.

Acupuncture research has reported effects on autonomic nervous system activity, neurotransmitters, and endocrine markers; how those findings map onto jet lag in real travelers is still being studied. JetLagPro supports an observational research program (pre-registered on OSF) that collects anonymous trip and symptom data from consenting participants. Chronoacupuncture vs. Other Jet Lag Approaches

Most travelers combine several strategies. None is a magic switch; adjustment depends on how many time zones you cross, direction of travel, sleep debt, and individual biology.

  • Light exposure strongly shifts the master clock in the brain; timing differs for eastward vs. westward travel.
  • Melatonin can signal “biological night” when timed carefully; dose and timing matter more than brand.
  • Meal and sleep scheduling help peripheral organ clocks catch up to local time.
  • Timed acupressure adds a structured, repeating somatosensory routine tied to destination time—a approach acupuncturists have used for decades and that JetLagPro now automates.

Many JetLagPro users also follow conventional travel-hygiene advice; the app is designed to fit alongside those habits, not replace medical guidance.
