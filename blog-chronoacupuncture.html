What Is Timed Acupressure?

May 30, 2026 • By Dr. Steven Schram, Licensed Acupuncturist

Timed acupressure means pressing specific points on a schedule tied to the clock at your destination, not your departure city. You may also see the research term chronoacupuncture for the same idea. This article explains what that means in plain language, where the schedule comes from, and how the JetLagPro app guides it as self-administered acupressure (no needles).

A Simple Definition

Timed acupressure for travel combines two ideas:

  • Time: the schedule follows the destination time zone, not the time on your wrist at departure.
  • Acupressure: firm pressure or tapping on the skin at known points—no needles.

The day is split into twelve two-hour blocks. Each block has one key point to press. During a long flight or after arrival, you use the point that matches “what time it is there,” roughly every two hours.

JetLagPro is built around that schedule: pick your destination, and the app reminds you which point to use for the current window, with videos and diagrams so you do not need prior training.

Where Did This Come From?

Traditional Chinese medicine describes a 24-hour rhythm in which different body systems peak at predictable times. In the late twentieth century, acupuncturists including Dr. John A. Amaro published jet-lag protocols: set a clock to destination time, and at each two-hour interval press the key point for that window—usually on both sides of the body. JetLagPro automates the timing and point guidance so you are not doing time-zone math while exhausted at 35,000 feet.

How It Works on a Trip

You follow destination local time, press the active point when prompted (left and right sides), and repeat on each two-hour handoff through travel and often into the first day after arrival. Timing matters as much as which point you use—that is why the app anchors everything to where you are going, not where you left from.

How This Relates to Jet Lag and Circadian Rhythm

Jet lag is a mismatch between your internal clocks and local sun time. Light, meals, sleep, and melatonin are well-known cues that tell the brain and organs what “time” it is. Timed acupressure adds another: repeated pressure on a schedule aligned with destination time.

How acupressure maps onto jet lag in real travelers is still being studied. JetLagPro supports an observational research program on iPhone (pre-registered on OSF) that collects anonymous trip and symptom data from consenting participants. The app is instructional; it is not a medical device or a substitute for professional care.

Timed Acupressure vs. Other Jet Lag Approaches

Most travelers combine several strategies. None is a magic switch; adjustment depends on how many time zones you cross, direction of travel, sleep debt, and individual biology.

  • Light exposure strongly shifts the master clock in the brain; timing differs for eastward vs. westward travel.
  • Melatonin can signal “biological night” when timed carefully; dose and timing matter more than brand.
  • Meal and sleep scheduling help your body catch up to local time.
  • Timed acupressure adds a structured, repeating routine tied to destination time—an approach acupuncturists have used for decades and that JetLagPro now automates.

Many JetLagPro users also follow conventional travel-hygiene advice; the app is designed to fit alongside those habits, not replace medical guidance.
